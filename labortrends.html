Labor Trends

Publishing

One major aspect our professional talked about was the consistent importance of publishing, which started during her graduate degree and has continued into her current work description/job expectations. While this is part of our professional’s contract, there is not enough time in a normal workday to pursue writing, so these responsibilities must be performed on our professional’s own time. We expect that this will be a consistent expectation in the GLAM field.

Travel

Part of our professional’s job is travelling, which we have mentioned previously in the dossier. People interested in pursuing this field should expect to travel as part of their work, especially if they plan to pursue a career with donor engagement.

Communication

As mentioned above, our professional engages in communication with colleagues and donors outside of normal work hours. Part of this is due to naturally forming friendships with people in the same field.

Relationship Building

Our interview subject also functions as an artist liaison; a large part of their job is facilitating and maintaining relationships with donors. They directly work with artists contributing their work to the archive, which requires the building of trust and rapport with said donors.

Public Facing

Due to the shrinking of teams within archives, our professional noted that archivists are to take on multiple roles including that of a curator and archivist. It is essential that archivists are able to work with the public; our interview subjects’ background in community archives equipped them to navigate different dynamics within the community.

Rise of the private archivist

With more repositories closing and overall job inconsistency (being hired based on workload), our interview subject noted there was a trend towards private archivists. They are hired on a project-to-project basis, usually as a consultant. Private archivists seem to be a natural response to the gig-economy.
